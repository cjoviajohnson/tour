Calicut

Kozhikode, commonly known as Calicut in English, is a city in the Indian state of Kerala that is located along the Malabar Coast. It is the second biggest metropolitan region in Kerala and the 19th largest in India, with a corporate limit population of 609,224[6] and a metropolitan population of more than 2 million. Kozhikode is categorised by the Indian government as a Tier 2 city. It served as the capital of the Malabar district during the British rule and is the biggest city in the Malabar area. Because it served as the primary commercial hub for Indian spices during antiquity and the Middle Ages, Kozhikode was known as the "City of Spices." It served as the seat of government for a separate kingdom controlled by the Samoothiris.

Major Atrractions

Kozhikode Beach

Kozhikode Beach or Calicut Beach is a beach on the western side of Kozhikode, situated on the Malabar Coast of India. The beach is accessible through four road overbridges in the city. The beach has paved stones and illumination. There is one Lions Park for the children and an aquarium. Kozhikode beach has always been a prominent place for conducting public meetings. The beach road was renamed 'Gandhi Road' in 1934 after Gandhi visited Calicut in 1934.

Pazhassiraja Archaeological Museum

In Kozhikode, Kerala, there is a museum and art gallery called the Pazhassiraja Archaeological Museum. From 1000 BC to 200 AD, the museum's extensive collection of historical objects is on display. East Hill Bungalow, the original name of the structure that currently houses the museum, was built in 1812. In 1976, the cottage underwent conversion to become an archaeological museum. The structure was given a new name in 1980: Pazhassi Raja Archaeological Museum.

Mishkal Mosque

Mishkal Mosque , is a medieval mosque located in Calicut on Malabar Coast, southern India. The mosque, one of the few surviving medieval mosques in Kerala, is regarded as an important cultural, historical and architectural monument of Kerala. The mosque was built by the eponymous Muslim merchant-shipowner (nakhuda) in the 14th century. Mishkal - active in Calicut in the 1340s - possessed "great wealth" and a fleet of ships for "the trade with India, China, Yemen, and Persia".
